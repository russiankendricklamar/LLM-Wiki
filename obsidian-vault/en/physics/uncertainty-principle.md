# Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

## Theoretical Foundation
The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle is a cornerstone of quantum mechanics, stating that there is a fundamental limit to the precision with which certain pairs of physical properties, known as complementary variables (such as position and momentum), can be known simultaneously. This is not a statement about the limitations of measurement instruments, but rather an intrinsic property of quantum systems arising from the geometry of the Hilbert space and the non-commutativity of the associated Hermitian operators.

## Mathematical Formulation
For any two observables represented by operators $\hat{A}$ and $\hat{B}$, the generalized uncertainty principle (Robertson-Schrödinger relation) is:
$$ \sigma_A \sigma_B \ge \frac{1}{2} |\langle [\hat{A}, \hat{B}] \rangle| $$
For position $x$ and momentum $p$, this reduces to:
$$ \sigma_x \sigma_p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} $$

## Physical Significance & Information Theory
In quantum information theory, the uncertainty principle represents a lower bound on the entropy of measurement outcomes. Entropic uncertainty relations provide a more robust formulation, linking the uncertainties of non-commuting observables to the Shannon or von Neumann entropy. Furthermore, generalized uncertainty principles (GUP) are proposed in quantum gravity, suggesting a minimum measurable length (Planck length), which fundamentally alters the structure of phase space and regulates ultraviolet divergences in quantum field theories.
