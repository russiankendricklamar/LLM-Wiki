# Superposition & Collapse

## Theoretical Foundation
Quantum superposition posits that any two (or more) quantum states can be added together ("superposed") and the result will be another valid quantum state. This is a direct consequence of the linearity of the Hilbert space. Wave function collapse (or state reduction) is the transition from a superposition of eigenstates to a single eigenstate upon measurement. This non-unitary, non-linear process poses the fundamental "measurement problem" in quantum mechanics, contrasting sharply with the deterministic unitary evolution governed by the [[schrodinger-equation]].

## Mathematical Formulation
A general state vector $|\psi\rangle$ can be expanded in an orthonormal basis $\{|a_i\rangle\}$ of an observable $\hat{A}$:
$$ |\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |a_i\rangle $$
Upon measuring $\hat{A}$, the state collapses to $|a_k\rangle$ with probability $P_k = |c_k|^2$. After measurement, the state is discontinuously updated:
$$ |\psi\rangle \xrightarrow{\text{measurement}} |a_k\rangle $$

## Physical Significance & Holography
The tension between unitary evolution and non-unitary collapse is central to debates in quantum foundations and quantum gravity. In holographic duality, the interior of a black hole and its relation to the exterior raises questions about whether the collapse of the state is an objective physical process or a subjective updating of information (as in quantum Bayesianism). Decoherence theory explains the appearance of collapse via entanglement with the environment, deeply linking superposition to the spread of quantum information.
