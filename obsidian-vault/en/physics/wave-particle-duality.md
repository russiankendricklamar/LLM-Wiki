# Wave-Particle Duality

## Theoretical Foundation
Wave-particle duality is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics asserting that every particle or quantum entity may be described as either a particle or a wave. In the context of the abstract Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$, the state of a system $|\psi\rangle$ is a vector that contains all probabilistic information about the system. The physical manifestation of this state depends entirely on the observable being measured, dictated by non-commuting Hermitian operators. This duality resolves classical contradictions by introducing a unified framework where position (particle-like) and momentum (wave-like) are Fourier conjugates.

## Mathematical Formulation
The duality is mathematically encapsulated by the de Broglie relations, connecting the momentum $\mathbf{p}$ and energy $E$ of a particle to the wavevector $\mathbf{k}$ and angular frequency $\omega$ of its associated wave:
$$ \mathbf{p} = \hbar \mathbf{k}, \quad E = \hbar \omega $$
The wave nature of matter implies that the commutator of the position $\hat{x}$ and momentum $\hat{p}$ operators is non-zero:
$$ [\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar $$

## Physical Significance & Information Theory
In the realm of quantum information theory, wave-particle duality is intimately related to the concept of complementarity and information gain. Extracting which-path information (particle nature) strictly bounds the visibility of interference fringes (wave nature), a relationship formalized by the Englert-Greenberger duality relation: $D^2 + V^2 \le 1$, where $D$ is distinguishability and $V$ is visibility. This fundamental limit on information extraction connects deeply to the [[uncertainty-principle]] and bounds the capacities of quantum channels.
