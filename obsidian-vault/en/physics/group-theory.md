# Group Theory in Physics

## Lie Groups and Lie Algebras
Symmetries in physics are described using Lie groups. A Lie group $G$ is a differentiable manifold that also has a group structure. The infinitesimal generators $T^a$ form a Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$:
$$ [T^a, T^b] = i f^{abc} T^c $$
where $f^{abc}$ are the structure constants.

## Representation Theory
A representation maps group elements to operators acting on a Hilbert space. In quantum mechanics, particles transform under specific representations of symmetry groups:
* **Fundamental representation:** The smallest non-trivial representation.
* **Adjoint representation:** How the generators themselves transform.

## Unitary and Orthogonal Groups
* **$SU(n)$:** The group of $n \times n$ unitary matrices with determinant 1. $SU(2)$ describes spin, while $SU(3)$ is the gauge group of Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD).
* **$SO(n)$:** The group of $n \times n$ orthogonal matrices with determinant 1, describing rotations in $n$-dimensional space.

## Gauge Symmetries
Gauge theories are based on local symmetry groups. The Standard Model is built on the gauge group $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$. The requirement of local gauge invariance necessitates the introduction of gauge fields (bosons).
