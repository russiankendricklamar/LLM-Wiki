# Asymptotic Spacetime

## Theoretical Foundation
Asymptotic spacetime refers to the geometry of a manifold far from any localized sources of mass or energy. The most common case is "asymptotically flat" spacetime, which approaches the Minkowski metric at large distances. Understanding the structure of spacetime at "infinity" is crucial for defining physical quantities like total mass (ADM mass), momentum, and the radiation that escapes the system. The formal study involves Penrose's conformal compactification, which adds a "boundary" (null infinity $\mathcal{I}$) to the spacetime.

## Mathematical Formulation
A spacetime $(M, g)$ is asymptotically flat if there exists a conformal factor $\Omega$ such that the unphysical metric $\tilde{g} = \Omega^2 g$ is well-behaved at the boundary where $\Omega = 0$. In Bond-Sachs coordinates $(u, r, z, \bar{z})$, the metric near null infinity $\mathcal{I}^+$ takes the form:
$$ ds^2 = -du^2 - 2du dr + r^2 \gamma_{z\bar{z}} dz d\bar{z} + \frac{2m_B}{r} du^2 + r C_{zz} dz^2 + \dots $$
where $m_B(u, z, \bar{z})$ is the Bondi mass aspect and $C_{zz}$ is the news tensor describing gravitational radiation.

## Physical Significance & Holography
The structure of asymptotic spacetime is the stage for [[asymptotic-symmetries]] and the [[bms-group]]. It defines the "holographic screen" where celestial holography is formulated. The fact that the boundary is null (light-like) rather than spacelike (as in AdS/CFT) presents unique challenges and opportunities for defining a dual theory that captures the dynamics of gravity in our own seemingly flat universe.
