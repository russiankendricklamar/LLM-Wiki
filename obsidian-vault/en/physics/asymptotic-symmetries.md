# Asymptotic Symmetries

## Theoretical Foundation
Asymptotic symmetries are the symmetries of a spacetime that persist at its boundary (null infinity $\mathcal{I}$ for asymptotically flat spacetimes). Unlike local gauge transformations which are redundant descriptions, asymptotic symmetries are "large" gauge transformations that act non-trivially on the physical state of the system. In 4D flat spacetime, these symmetries form the [[bms-group]], which is infinitely larger than the standard Poincaré group, indicating that the vacuum of general relativity is not unique but infinitely degenerate.

## Mathematical Formulation
An asymptotic symmetry is defined by a diffeomorphism $\xi$ that preserves the asymptotic form of the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$. In Bond-Sachs coordinates, the conditions are:
$$ \mathcal{L}_\xi g_{\mu\nu} \sim \mathcal{O}(\text{decay rates}) $$
The generators of these symmetries include supertranslations $f(z, \bar{z})$ and superrotations $Y^z(z)$. The associated charges $Q_\xi$ are calculated using the surface integral:
$$ Q_\xi = \frac{1}{8\pi G} \int_{\partial \Sigma} \star \mathcal{F}(\xi) $$
where $\star \mathcal{F}(\xi)$ is the 2-form associated with the symmetry generator.

## Physical Significance & Holography
Asymptotic symmetries provide the symmetry group for the boundary theory in holography. For asymptotically flat spacetimes, this leads to the framework of [[celestial-holography]], where the 4D physics is mapped to a 2D theory on the celestial sphere. These symmetries are essential for the conservation of "soft" charges, which play a crucial role in ensuring that information is not lost in scattering processes, directly addressing the foundations of the Black Hole Information Paradox.
