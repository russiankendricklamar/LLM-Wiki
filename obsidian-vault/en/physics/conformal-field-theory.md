# Conformal Field Theory (CFT)

## Theoretical Foundation
Conformal Field Theory (CFT) is a quantum field theory invariant under conformal transformations — angle-preserving maps that include translations, rotations, dilations, and special conformal transformations. The conformal group in $d$ dimensions is $SO(d+1,1)$ for Euclidean signature. In $d = 2$, the conformal algebra becomes infinite-dimensional, generated by local holomorphic and anti-holomorphic vector fields, giving 2D CFT exceptional computational power.

CFTs arise naturally at the fixed points of the renormalization group (RG) flow, describing second-order phase transitions where the correlation length diverges and the system becomes scale invariant. The Ising model at its critical temperature, free massless fields, and WZW models are classic examples. Beyond statistical mechanics, CFTs appear as worldsheet theories of strings, as the boundary theories in AdS/CFT, and as the celestial dual in flat-space holography.

The modern bootstrap program — initiated by Rattazzi, Rychkov, Tonni, and Vichi (2008) — uses crossing symmetry and unitarity to constrain CFT data (operator dimensions and OPE coefficients) to remarkable precision without perturbation theory, determining critical exponents of the 3D Ising model to ten significant figures.

## Mathematical Formulation
The generators of the 2D conformal algebra satisfy the Virasoro algebra. The modes $L_n$ and $\bar{L}_n$ of the stress tensor $T(z)$ and $\bar{T}(\bar{z})$ obey:
$$ [L_m, L_n] = (m-n)L_{m+n} + \frac{c}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0} $$
$$ [\bar{L}_m, \bar{L}_n] = (m-n)\bar{L}_{m+n} + \frac{\bar{c}}{12}m(m^2-1)\delta_{m+n,0} $$
$$ [L_m, \bar{L}_n] = 0 $$
where $c$ ($\bar{c}$) is the holomorphic (anti-holomorphic) central charge. The central charge measures the quantum anomaly of conformal symmetry and equals the number of free scalar fields for a free theory.

Primary operators $\mathcal{O}_{h,\bar{h}}(z,\bar{z})$ with conformal weights $(h,\bar{h})$ transform as:
$$ \mathcal{O}_{h,\bar{h}}(z,\bar{z}) \to \left(\frac{\partial z'}{\partial z}\right)^h \left(\frac{\partial \bar{z}'}{\partial \bar{z}}\right)^{\bar{h}} \mathcal{O}_{h,\bar{h}}(z',\bar{z}') $$
The scaling dimension is $\Delta = h + \bar{h}$ and the spin is $s = h - \bar{h}$.

The Operator Product Expansion (OPE) encodes the algebra of local operators:
$$ \mathcal{O}_i(z,\bar{z})\mathcal{O}_j(0,0) = \sum_k C_{ij}^k\, z^{h_k - h_i - h_j}\bar{z}^{\bar{h}_k - \bar{h}_i - \bar{h}_j}\mathcal{O}_k(0,0) $$
The OPE coefficients $C_{ij}^k$ together with the set of operator dimensions $\{(\Delta_k, s_k)\}$ constitute the CFT data, which completely determines all correlation functions via conformal Ward identities.

Two- and three-point functions are fully fixed by conformal symmetry:
$$ \langle\mathcal{O}_i(z)\mathcal{O}_j(w)\rangle = \frac{\delta_{ij}}{|z-w|^{2\Delta_i}} $$
$$ \langle\mathcal{O}_1\mathcal{O}_2\mathcal{O}_3\rangle = \frac{C_{123}}{|z_{12}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_2-\Delta_3}|z_{23}|^{\Delta_2+\Delta_3-\Delta_1}|z_{13}|^{\Delta_1+\Delta_3-\Delta_2}} $$

## Key Properties
- **State-operator correspondence**: In 2D CFT, every state in the Hilbert space on $S^1$ corresponds to a local operator at the origin via the radial quantization map $z = e^{i(\tau + \sigma)}$.
- **Modular invariance**: The torus partition function $Z(\tau) = \text{Tr}(q^{L_0 - c/24}\bar{q}^{\bar{L}_0 - \bar{c}/24})$ must be invariant under modular transformations $\tau \to -1/\tau$, constraining the spectrum.
- **Cardy formula**: The density of states at high energy follows $\ln \rho(E) \sim 2\pi\sqrt{cE/3}$, reproducing the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy when the CFT is dual to AdS$_3$ gravity.
- **Unitarity bounds**: In unitary CFTs, scaling dimensions satisfy $\Delta \geq (d-2)/2$ for scalars, bounding the operator spectrum from below.
- **Conformal blocks**: Four-point functions decompose into conformal partial waves (conformal blocks) $\mathcal{G}_{\Delta,s}(z,\bar{z})$, which are kinematically determined and encode the exchange of a single primary and all its descendants.

## Physical Interpretation
The central charge $c$ measures the number of degrees of freedom; more precisely, it appears in the Weyl anomaly $\langle T^\mu_\mu \rangle = -cR/12$ on a curved background. The $c$-theorem (Zamolodchikov, 1986) states that $c$ decreases along RG flows from UV to IR fixed points, providing a quantitative measure of how degrees of freedom are lost in renormalization.

The bootstrap philosophy is that all CFTs are defined by their CFT data, not by a Lagrangian. Crossing symmetry — the requirement that four-point functions can be decomposed in different channels consistently — is a powerful constraint that, combined with unitarity, severely limits which CFTs can exist. This non-perturbative approach has solved previously intractable problems.

## Connections to Information Theory & Holography
CFT is the boundary theory in AdS/CFT: a $d$-dimensional CFT is dual to $(d+1)$-dimensional gravity in anti-de Sitter space. The CFT partition function equals the gravitational partition function: $Z_{\rm CFT} = Z_{\rm gravity}$. Operator dimensions correspond to bulk particle masses via $\Delta(\Delta - d) = m^2 L^2$. Entanglement entropy in the CFT equals the Ryu-Takayanagi minimal surface area in the bulk.

In [[celestial-holography]], the celestial sphere hosts a 2D CFT with the Virasoro symmetry extended to $w_{1+\infty}$. Unlike standard 2D CFTs, the celestial CFT is non-unitary and has a continuous spectrum, but the same OPE and crossing-symmetry techniques apply, making CFT the universal language of holography.
