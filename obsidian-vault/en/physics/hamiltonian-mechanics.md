# Hamiltonian Mechanics

**Hamiltonian mechanics** is a reformulation of classical mechanics in which the evolution of a system is described by a single scalar function — the **Hamiltonian** $H(q, p, t)$ — and a pair of symmetric first-order equations. Developed by Hamilton in the 1830s as a reinterpretation of Lagrangian mechanics, it has become the geometric language of classical physics, from which Liouville's theorem, perturbation theory, quantisation, and modern numerical integrators all naturally emerge.

## Phase space

In Lagrangian mechanics the state is given by generalised coordinates $q_i$ and velocities $\dot q_i$; in Hamiltonian mechanics, by coordinates $q_i$ and **canonically conjugate momenta**

$$
p_i = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q_i}.
$$

The space of all $(q, p)$ pairs is **phase space** $\mathcal{M}$ of dimension $2n$ for a system with $n$ degrees of freedom. The Hamiltonian is built from the Lagrangian via the **Legendre transform**:

$$
H(q, p, t) = \sum_i p_i \dot q_i - L(q, \dot q, t),
$$

with the right-hand side expressed in terms of $p$. For conservative systems, $H$ coincides with the total energy.

## Hamilton's equations

Evolution in phase space is governed by symmetric equations:

$$
\dot q_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot p_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}.
$$

In vector form with $\mathbf z = (q, p)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{2n}$:

$$
\dot{\mathbf z} = J \,\nabla H, \qquad J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

The matrix $J$ — the **symplectic form** — is antisymmetric and satisfies $J^2 = -I$. It encodes the non-Euclidean geometry of phase space: coordinates and momenta are not orthogonal axes, but conjugate pairs.

## Energy conservation and Poisson brackets

If $H$ does not depend explicitly on time, the total derivative along a trajectory is

$$
\dot H = \nabla H \cdot \dot{\mathbf z} = \nabla H \cdot J \nabla H = 0,
$$

because $J$ is antisymmetric. **Energy is conserved automatically** — not as an external law but as an identity of symplectic geometry.

The **Poisson bracket** of two functions $f, g$ on phase space is

$$
\{f, g\} = \sum_i \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial q_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q_i} \right).
$$

The evolution of any observable $f(q, p, t)$ is

$$
\dot f = \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}.
$$

A quantity is conserved iff $\{f, H\} = 0$ and $f$ has no explicit time dependence. This relates symmetries to conservation laws (Noether's theorem in Hamiltonian language).

## Liouville's theorem

The flow of a Hamiltonian system **preserves phase-space volume**: if $\rho(q, p, t)$ is the density of an ensemble of systems, then

$$
\frac{d\rho}{dt} = 0.
$$

Geometrically, the symplectic 2-form $\omega = \sum_i dp_i \wedge dq_i$ and its exterior powers $\omega^n / n!$ (the volume form) are invariant under Hamiltonian flow. This identity is the foundation of **statistical mechanics** and ergodic theory.

## Canonical transformations

Changes of variables $(q, p) \to (Q, P)$ that preserve the form of Hamilton's equations are called **canonical**. They are generated by functions $F(q, Q)$, $F(q, P)$, etc. Canonical transformations are **symplectomorphisms**: they preserve the symplectic form $\omega$.

A key special case is the **Hamilton-Jacobi transformation**, which carries the system into trivial dynamics ($\dot Q = 0$, $\dot P = 0$). The corresponding generating function $S(q, P, t)$ satisfies the Hamilton-Jacobi equation:

$$
H\left(q, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q}, t\right) + \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} = 0.
$$

This is a PDE, and one of the gateways to quantum mechanics: substituting $\psi = e^{iS/\hbar}$ into the [[schrodinger-equation|Schrödinger equation]] recovers Hamilton-Jacobi in the classical limit $\hbar \to 0$.

## Examples

**1D harmonic oscillator.** $H = p^2/(2m) + m\omega^2 q^2/2$. Trajectories are ellipses in the phase plane.

**Pendulum.** $H = p^2/(2ml^2) - mgl\cos q$. Phase space is split by a separatrix into librations (oscillations) and rotations.

**Kepler problem.** $H = p^2/(2m) - GMm/r$. An integrable system with three conserved quantities (energy, and the magnitude and direction of angular momentum), which explains why bound orbits are closed ellipses.

**Chaotic systems.** The double pendulum, three-body problem, and billiards are Hamiltonian but non-integrable. The KAM theorem describes the breakdown of invariant tori under perturbation.

## Symplectic integrators

Numerically integrating Hamiltonian systems with standard methods (Runge-Kutta) leads to **energy drift** — the solution leaves the constant-$H$ surface. **Symplectic integrators** preserve the symplectic form by construction and therefore conserve energy (up to small oscillations around the true value) on exponentially long timescales.

Classic examples:

- **Symplectic Euler** (first order).
- **Leapfrog / Störmer-Verlet** (second order): the de facto standard in molecular dynamics.
- **Yoshida integrators** of fourth and higher order.

Precisely this property — preserving symplectic structure — makes neural approximators of the Hamiltonian — [[hamiltonian-nn|Hamiltonian Neural Networks]] — fundamentally better long-horizon predictors than [[neural-odes|Neural ODEs]] that learn a generic vector field.

## Link to quantum mechanics

**Canonical quantisation** replaces classical variables with operators: $q \to \hat q$, $p \to -i\hbar \partial_q$, and Poisson brackets with commutators: $\{f, g\} \to (i/\hbar)[\hat f, \hat g]$. The correspondence principle ensures classical and quantum predictions agree in the limit of large quantum numbers.

Hence the tight link with the [[uncertainty-principle]] — the canonical relation $[\hat q, \hat p] = i\hbar$ follows directly from the symplectic geometry of phase space.
