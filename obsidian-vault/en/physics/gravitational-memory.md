# Gravitational Memory

## Theoretical Foundation
Gravitational memory is a non-linear, permanent change in the relative displacement of test masses following the passage of a gravitational wave burst. It arises from the fact that gravitational radiation carries away not just energy, but also information about the source's mass-energy distribution. This effect is a low-frequency, "DC" component of gravitational waves that remains long after the oscillatory part has passed, representing a transition between different vacua of the spacetime.

## Mathematical Formulation
The memory effect is related to the change in the transverse-traceless (TT) part of the metric $h_{ij}^{TT}$. In the asymptotic limit, the memory $\Delta h_{ij}^{TT}$ is given by:
$$ \Delta h_{ij}^{TT} = \lim_{t \to \infty} h_{ij}^{TT}(t) - \lim_{t \to -\infty} h_{ij}^{TT}(t) $$
The leading order memory is proportional to the total change in the quadrupole moment of the source, often expressed as an integral over the flux of energy-momentum to null infinity $\mathcal{I}^+$:
$$ \Delta h_{ij} \sim \int du \frac{d^2 E}{d\Omega du} $$

## Physical Significance & Information Theory
Gravitational memory is one corner of "Pasterski’s Triangle," linking it to [[soft-theorems]] and [[asymptotic-symmetries]]. It represents the physical manifestation of the vacuum transition caused by large gauge transformations. In the context of the Black Hole Information Paradox, memory effects are proposed as a mechanism for storing information in "soft hair" on the event horizon, suggesting that the vacuum of general relativity is infinitely degenerate and can carry information about the past history of the system.
