# Memory Effects

## Theoretical Foundation
Memory effects refer to the permanent changes in the state of a detector or the geometry of spacetime after the passage of a radiation burst (gravitational, electromagnetic, or color radiation). These effects are infrared phenomena, occurring at zero frequency. While [[gravitational-memory]] is the most famous, there exist analogous effects in gauge theories, such as the electromagnetic memory (a permanent shift in the velocity of a charged particle) and the color memory in QCD.

## Mathematical Formulation
The general structure of a memory effect $\Delta \mathcal{O}$ is the integral of a flux $\mathcal{F}$ over time:
$$ \Delta \mathcal{O} = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt \mathcal{F}(t) $$
In electromagnetism, the memory $\Delta \mathbf{v}$ for a particle of charge $e$ and mass $m$ due to a radiation field $\mathbf{E}(t)$ is:
$$ \Delta \mathbf{v} = \frac{e}{m} \int dt \mathbf{E}(t) $$
This corresponds to the "soft" limit of the radiated field's Fourier transform, linking the effect directly to [[soft-theorems]].

## Physical Significance & Information Theory
Memory effects are the physical observables that verify the existence of [[asymptotic-symmetries]]. They represent a transition between different vacuum states of the theory. From an information-theoretic perspective, memory effects encode the total amount of "information" (integrated flux) that has escaped to infinity. This makes them central to the study of the infrared structure of gravity and the proposed storage of information in the "soft hair" of black holes.
