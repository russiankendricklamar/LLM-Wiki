# Quantum Entanglement

## Theoretical Foundation
Quantum entanglement is a physical phenomenon where the quantum states of two or more objects cannot be described independently of the state of the others, even when the objects are separated by a large distance. The state resides in a tensor product Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B$. Entangled states represent non-local correlations that violate Bell's inequalities, demonstrating that quantum mechanics cannot be described by any local hidden-variable theory.

## Mathematical Formulation
A bipartite pure state $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B$ is entangled if it cannot be written as a separable product state:
$$ |\psi\rangle \neq |\phi_A\rangle \otimes |\phi_B\rangle $$
The degree of entanglement can be quantified using the von Neumann entropy of the reduced density matrix $\rho_A = \text{Tr}_B(|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|)$:
$$ S_E = - \text{Tr}(\rho_A \ln \rho_A) $$

## Physical Significance & Holography
Entanglement is the foundational currency of quantum information theory. In modern theoretical physics, it is conjectured to be the fundamental fabric of spacetime itself. The ER=EPR conjecture posits that entangled particles are connected by microscopic Einstein-Rosen bridges (wormholes). Furthermore, the Ryu-Takayanagi formula in AdS/CFT relates the entanglement entropy of a boundary CFT region to the area of a minimal bulk surface, directly translating quantum information into spacetime geometry.
