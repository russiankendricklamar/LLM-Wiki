# Types of Swaps

A **Swap** is a derivative financial instrument in which two parties exchange the cash flows or liabilities from two different financial instruments. Most swaps are traded over-the-counter (OTC) and are customized between the counterparties.

## 1. Interest Rate Swap (IRS)

The most common type of swap. Parties exchange interest rate payments: one party pays a **fixed rate**, while the other pays a **floating rate** (e.g., SOFR, EURIBOR, or LIBOR).

### Use Cases:
- Corporations with floating-rate debt can "lock in" their interest expenses if they expect rates to rise.
- Banks use IRS for Asset-Liability Management (ALM) to match interest rate sensitivities.

## 2. Currency Swap

The exchange of principal and interest payments in different currencies.
- **Cross-Currency Swap**: Exchanging a fixed rate in one currency for a floating rate in another.
- **FX Swap**: A short-term transaction combining a spot currency purchase and a forward sale.

## 3. Credit Default Swap (CDS)

A financial contract that acts as insurance against the default of an issuer. The CDS buyer pays a periodic premium to the seller, who agrees to compensate the buyer (usually the face value of the bond) if a credit event (default) occurs.

## 4. Total Return Swap (TRS)

One party (the total return receiver) receives all income generated by an asset (dividends + capital gains) and pays the other party (the owner) a fixed or floating interest rate. This allows for exposure to an asset without physically owning it.

## 5. Equity Swap

The exchange of the return on an equity or an equity index (e.g., S&P 500) for a fixed or floating interest rate. Often used by hedge funds to manage portfolio exposures efficiently.

## Mathematical Valuation (IRS)

The value of an Interest Rate Swap for the fixed-rate payer at time $t$ is the difference between the present value of the floating leg and the fixed leg:

$$
V_{swap} = PV_{float} - PV_{fixed}
$$

Where:
- $PV_{fixed} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} K \cdot N \cdot \Delta_i \cdot D(t_i)$
- $K$ is the fixed rate.
- $N$ is the notional amount.
- $D(t_i)$ is the discount factor for time $t_i$.

## Python: Calculating Fixed Leg PV

```python
def calculate_fixed_leg(notional, rate, tenors, discount_factors):
    """
    Present value of the fixed leg of an interest rate swap.
    """
    leg_pv = sum(notional * rate * delta * df 
                 for delta, df in zip(tenors, discount_factors))
    return leg_pv

# Example: 1 year, semi-annual payments
notional = 10_000_000
rate = 0.05
deltas = [0.5, 0.5]
dfs = [0.975, 0.951] # Hypothetical discount factors

pv = calculate_fixed_leg(notional, rate, deltas, dfs)
print(f"Fixed Leg PV: ${pv:,.2f}")
```

## Comparison of Major Swap Types

| Swap Type | What is Exchanged? | Primary Risk |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **IRS** | Fixed vs Floating rates | Interest Rate Risk |
| **Currency** | Currencies + Rates | FX + Interest Rate Risk |
| **CDS** | Premium vs Default Protection | Credit Risk |
| **TRS** | Asset Return vs Interest Rate | Asset Market Risk |
| **Commodity** | Commodity Price vs Rate | Commodity Price Risk |
| **Variance** | Realized vs Implied Volatility | Volatility Risk |
