# Local Limit Theorems

The [[central-limit-theorem|Central Limit Theorem]] describes convergence of **distribution functions** — it tells us that $P(S_n \leq x)$ is close to $\Phi(x)$. But in many applications one needs something finer: convergence of **densities** or **point probabilities**. Local limit theorems provide exactly this. They assert that not merely cumulative probabilities, but the probability mass or density at individual points converges to the corresponding Gaussian value. Borovkov develops the lattice and non-lattice cases in parallel, emphasizing the role of Cramer's condition on the characteristic function.

## The de Moivre-Laplace theorem

The oldest and most intuitive local limit theorem concerns the binomial distribution. Let $S_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$ with $q = 1 - p$.

**Theorem (de Moivre-Laplace, local form).** Uniformly in integer $k$ satisfying $|k - np| \leq C\sqrt{n}$ for any fixed $C > 0$:

$$P(S_n = k) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi npq}} \, \exp\!\left(-\frac{(k - np)^2}{2npq}\right).$$

Equivalently, with $\varphi$ denoting the standard normal density:

$$P(S_n = k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{npq}} \, \varphi\!\left(\frac{k - np}{\sqrt{npq}}\right) + o\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\right).$$

This is much stronger than the integral CLT: it approximates individual probabilities, not just their sums. The proof proceeds by Stirling's formula applied to $\binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k}$.

**Proof sketch.** Write $P(S_n = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k}$ and apply Stirling: $m! \sim \sqrt{2\pi m}\,(m/e)^m$. After substituting and simplifying the ratio of factorials, one obtains $\binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k} \approx (2\pi npq)^{-1/2} \exp(-x^2/2)$ where $x = (k - np)/\sqrt{npq}$. The error is $O(1/n)$ uniformly in $|x| \leq C$, which can be made precise using the next term in Stirling's expansion.

## Gnedenko's local theorem for lattice distributions

A random variable $X$ has a **lattice distribution** with span $h > 0$ if its values lie in a set $\{a + kh : k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ and $h$ is maximal with this property.

**Theorem (Gnedenko, 1948).** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. lattice random variables with span $h$, mean $\mu$, and variance $\sigma^2 > 0$. Then uniformly in $k \in \mathbb{Z}$:

$$P(S_n = a_n + kh) = \frac{h}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi n}} \, \varphi\!\left(\frac{a_n + kh - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right) + o\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\right),$$

where $a_n$ is the appropriate lattice shift of $S_n$.

The proof uses the **inversion formula** for [[characteristic-functions|characteristic functions]]: the point probability is recovered by integrating the characteristic function over one period $[-\pi/h, \pi/h]$, then the main contribution comes from a neighborhood of zero where the CLT-type expansion applies.

## The non-lattice (density) case

When $X$ has a density (or more generally satisfies Cramer's condition), the local theorem takes a different form.

**Cramer's condition.** $\limsup_{|t| \to \infty} |\varphi_X(t)| < 1$, i.e., the characteristic function does not return to modulus 1 away from the origin.

**Theorem (non-lattice local CLT).** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$, and suppose Cramer's condition holds. Then $S_n$ has a density $f_{S_n}$ for all large $n$, and

$$f_{S_n}(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \, \varphi\!\left(\frac{x - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right) + o\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\right)$$

uniformly in $x \in \mathbb{R}$.

Without Cramer's condition the density may not exist or may oscillate, and no uniform local theorem is possible.

## Stone's general local theorem

Stone (1965) unified and extended the theory by proving a local limit theorem for arbitrary distributions on $\mathbb{R}^d$ under minimal assumptions. The key insight: replace point probabilities by probabilities of intervals whose length tends to zero slowly enough.

**Theorem (Stone).** Under suitable non-lattice conditions, for any bounded measurable set $A$ with negligible boundary:

$$n^{d/2} \, P\!\left(\frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \in A\right) \to \int_A \varphi(x) \, dx.$$

This covers both the lattice and non-lattice cases as special instances. Stone's theorem is particularly important in higher dimensions ($d \geq 2$), where the lattice/non-lattice dichotomy becomes more subtle and the notion of "span" must be replaced by the concept of the lattice generated by the support of the distribution.

## Rates of convergence

Edgeworth expansions provide refined asymptotics beyond the leading Gaussian term. Under the existence of $m \geq 3$ moments:

$$f_{S_n}(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \left[\varphi(z) + \sum_{k=1}^{m-2} \frac{P_k(z)}{\,n^{k/2}\,} \, \varphi(z) \right] + o\!\left(n^{-(m-1)/2}\right),$$

where $z = (x - n\mu)/(\sigma\sqrt{n})$ and $P_k$ are polynomials determined by the cumulants of $X$. The first correction term involves the **skewness** $\gamma_1 = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^3]/\sigma^3$ and is of order $1/\sqrt{n}$.

## Applications

**Combinatorics and asymptotic enumeration.** The number of ways to represent an integer as a sum of parts drawn from a given set is a convolution — its asymptotics follow from local limit theorems. The celebrated Hardy-Ramanujan formula for the partition function $p(n) \sim \frac{1}{4n\sqrt{3}} e^{\pi\sqrt{2n/3}}$ is at heart a saddle-point argument, which is the analytic cousin of a local CLT applied to the generating function.

**Random walks on lattices.** The probability that a simple random walk returns to the origin after $2n$ steps: $P(S_{2n} = 0) \sim 1/\sqrt{\pi n}$ in dimension 1, which is the local CLT for Bernoulli steps. The recurrence of random walks in $d \leq 2$ versus transience in $d \geq 3$ ultimately rests on the summability of these local probabilities.

**Statistical mechanics.** Partition functions in lattice models are often expressible as sums over paths, and their asymptotics rely on local CLT techniques applied to the transfer matrix [[spectral-theory-operators|eigenvalues]].

**Number theory.** Many additive number-theoretic functions (e.g., the number of prime divisors $\omega(n)$) satisfy an Erdos-Kac-type theorem — a CLT. The local version gives the density of integers $n \leq N$ with $\omega(n) = k$ for specific $k$.

## Local theorems for large deviations

When $k$ is not in the "normal zone" $|k - np| = O(\sqrt{n})$ but instead in the "large deviations zone" $k - np = O(n)$, local theorems take a different form. Borovkov shows that under Cramer's condition:

$$P(S_n = k) \sim \frac{h}{\sigma(a)\sqrt{2\pi n}} \exp\!\big(-n \Lambda^*(k/n)\big),$$

where $\Lambda^*(x) = \sup_t (tx - \Lambda(t))$ is the Legendre transform of the cumulant generating function $\Lambda(t) = \log \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$, and $\sigma(a)$ is a variance parameter at the tilted distribution. This connects local limit theorems to [[large-deviations-cramer|large deviations theory]] and is essential for precise asymptotics in rare-event simulation.

## Historical development

De Moivre proved the first local limit theorem for the symmetric case $p = 1/2$ around 1733, making it arguably the oldest result in probability theory. Laplace extended it to general $p$. Gnedenko's 1948 theorem unified the lattice theory, while Shepp (1964) and Stone (1965) handled the general case. The Russian school — Gnedenko, Kolmogorov, Prokhorov, and Borovkov — was particularly influential in developing the theory, with Borovkov's contributions to the large-deviations regime being especially notable.
