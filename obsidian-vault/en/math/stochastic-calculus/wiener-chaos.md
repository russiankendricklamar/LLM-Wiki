# Wiener Chaos and Multiple Stochastic Integrals

Wiener chaos represents a fundamental decomposition of the space of square-integrable random variables generated by Brownian motion. Developed by Norbert Wiener and Kiyosi Itô, this theory is the cornerstone of modern stochastic analysis and finds applications in quantitative finance, quantum field theory, and statistical mechanics.

## Hermite Polynomials and Orthogonality

Hermite polynomials form a complete orthogonal basis in $L^2(\gamma)$, where $\gamma$ is the standard Gaussian measure. They are defined via the Rodrigues formula:

$$H_n(x) = (-1)^n e^{x^2/2}\frac{d^n}{dx^n}e^{-x^2/2}$$

Examples:
- $H_0(x) = 1$
- $H_1(x) = x$
- $H_2(x) = x^2 - 1$
- $H_3(x) = x^3 - 3x$

They satisfy the recurrence: $H_{n+1}(x) = x H_n(x) - n H_{n-1}(x)$.

## First Wiener Chaos

The first Wiener chaos $\mathcal{H}_1$ consists of all linear functionals of Brownian motion. If $W_t$ is a standard Brownian motion on $[0,T]$, then $\mathcal{H}_1$ is isomorphic to $L^2([0,T])$:

$$\mathcal{H}_1 = \left\{ \int_0^T f(t) dW_t : f \in L^2([0,T]) \right\}$$

Elements of $\mathcal{H}_1$ are Gaussian random variables with zero mean and variance $\|f\|_{L^2}^2$.

## Multiple Stochastic Integrals

For a symmetric function $f \in L^2([0,T]^n)$, the multiple Itô stochastic integral is defined inductively:

$$I_n(f) = \int_0^T I_{n-1}(f(\cdot, t)) dW_t$$

Equivalently, it can be defined as an integral over the simplex $0 < t_n < \dots < t_1 < T$. The key property is the **Itô Isometry for multiple integrals**:

$$E[I_n(f)^2] = n! \|f\|_{L^2([0,T]^n)}^2$$

## Wiener-Itô Decomposition

The central result is the orthogonal decomposition of $L^2(\Omega)$:

$$L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{F}_W, P) = \bigoplus_{n=0}^\infty \mathcal{H}_n$$

Every $X \in L^2(\Omega)$ has a unique representation $X = \sum_{n=0}^\infty I_n(f_n)$, where $f_n$ are symmetric kernels. This is the stochastic analogue of a Fourier series.

## Malliavin Derivative

The Malliavin derivative $D$ acts as an inverse to the multiple integral. For $F = I_n(f)$:

$$D_t F = n I_{n-1}(f(\cdot, t))$$

It lowers the order of chaos by one. The **Clark-Ocone formula** provides a representation for any $F \in \mathbb{D}^{1,2}$:

$$F = E[F] + \int_0^T E[D_t F | \mathcal{F}_t] dW_t$$

## Fourth Moment Theorem (Nualart-Peccati)

A remarkable result stating that for a sequence of random variables $\{F_n\}$ in a fixed chaos, convergence in distribution to $N(0,1)$ is equivalent to $E[F_n^4] \to 3$. This links the fourth moment directly to the Central Limit Theorem within the chaos framework.
