# Skorokhod Topology and the Space D

The space $C[0,T]$ of continuous functions, equipped with the uniform topology, is the natural setting for convergence of continuous processes like [[brownian-motion|Brownian motion]]. But the moment we allow **jumps** — discrete-time approximations of Poisson processes, random walks converging to [[levy-processes|Levy processes]], empirical distribution functions — the uniform topology fails catastrophically. A sequence of step functions whose jump times converge to a limit will never converge uniformly to a function with a jump at that limit, because tiny shifts in jump location create $O(1)$ supremum-norm errors.

Skorokhod's insight was to equip the space of cadlag (right-continuous with left limits) functions with a topology that allows small perturbations of both **values** and **jump times**. This yields the Skorokhod space $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$, which is the foundation for all functional limit theorems in Jacod and Shiryaev's "Limit Theorems for Stochastic Processes."

## The Space $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$

**Definition.** $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$ is the set of all functions $x: [0,T] \to \mathbb{R}^d$ that are **cadlag**: right-continuous at every $t \in [0,T)$ with left limits at every $t \in (0,T]$.

Every cadlag function has at most countably many discontinuities. The jump at time $t$ is $\Delta x(t) = x(t) - x(t-)$. Continuous functions form the closed subspace $C[0,T] \subset \mathbb{D}[0,T]$.

## The J1 Topology (Skorokhod's Main Topology)

Let $\Lambda$ be the set of strictly increasing continuous bijections $\lambda: [0,T] \to [0,T]$ (time changes). The **J1 metric** is:

$$d_{J_1}(x, y) = \inf_{\lambda \in \Lambda} \left\{ \|\lambda - \mathrm{id}\|_\infty \vee \|x \circ \lambda - y\|_\infty \right\}.$$

Two functions are J1-close if one can be obtained from the other by a small time change combined with a small vertical perturbation. This is precisely the right notion: it identifies a step function jumping at time $t_n \to t_0$ with the step function jumping at $t_0$, provided the jump sizes converge.

**Key properties of $(\mathbb{D}[0,T], J_1)$:**

- It is a **separable complete metric space** (Polish space).
- $C[0,T]$ is a closed subspace, and the J1 topology restricted to $C[0,T]$ coincides with the uniform topology.
- Convergence $x_n \to x$ in J1 implies convergence at all continuity points of $x$.
- The Borel $\sigma$-algebra on $\mathbb{D}$ coincides with the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the coordinate projections $x \mapsto x(t)$.

## The M1 Topology

For some applications, J1 is too restrictive. If a single large jump "splits" into several smaller jumps in the approximation, J1 convergence fails even though the approximation is physically reasonable.

The **M1 topology** uses the concept of a **completed graph**: for each cadlag function $x$, form the graph by connecting $(t, x(t-))$ to $(t, x(t))$ with a vertical segment at each jump time. The M1 metric measures how close the completed graphs are as subsets of $[0,T] \times \mathbb{R}^d$, using a parametric representation.

$$d_{M_1}(x, y) = \inf_{(u,r)} \left\{ \|u_1 - u_2\|_\infty \vee \|r_1 - r_2\|_\infty \right\},$$

where the infimum runs over parametric representations of the completed graphs. M1 is strictly weaker than J1: every J1-convergent sequence is M1-convergent, but not conversely.

## Why C-Space Topology Is Insufficient

Consider the random walk $S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \xi_k$ with $\xi_k$ i.i.d., linearly interpolated to get a continuous process $X^{(n)}(t)$. Donsker's theorem gives $X^{(n)} \Rightarrow W$ in $C[0,1]$ (where $W$ is Brownian motion). This works because the limit is continuous.

Now replace $\xi_k$ by heavy-tailed random variables in the domain of attraction of a stable law. The limit process is a **stable Levy process** with jumps, living in $\mathbb{D}$, not $C$. If we tried to embed the piecewise-constant approximations in $C[0,1]$ (say, by linear interpolation), the jumps in the limit process would never be captured. Weak convergence in $\mathbb{D}$ with the J1 topology handles this naturally.

## Tightness and Relative Compactness

By Prokhorov's theorem (see [[weak-convergence-prokhorov]]), weak convergence of probability measures on a Polish space is equivalent to tightness plus convergence of finite-dimensional distributions. On $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$, tightness has concrete criteria.

**Theorem (Aldous-Rebolledo criterion).** A sequence of cadlag processes $(X^n)$ is tight in $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$ if and only if:

1. For each $t$, the sequence $(X^n_t)$ is tight in $\mathbb{R}^d$.
2. The **Aldous condition** holds: for every $\varepsilon > 0$ and $\eta > 0$, there exist $\delta > 0$ and $n_0$ such that for all $n \geq n_0$ and all stopping times $\tau_n \leq T$:
$$\sup_{\theta \leq \delta} P(|X^n_{\tau_n + \theta} - X^n_{\tau_n}| > \eta) < \varepsilon.$$

Condition (2) says that the process cannot have large oscillations over short time intervals, uniformly in the sequence. It is the stochastic analogue of equicontinuity.

**For semimartingales**, Jacod and Shiryaev reformulate tightness in terms of the **predictable characteristics** $(B^n, C^n, \nu^n)$: tightness of each component separately implies tightness of the process. This is the key technical device for proving limit theorems in their framework.

## Convergence of Discrete-Time to Continuous-Time Processes

The standard recipe for proving functional limit theorems via Skorokhod topology:

1. **Embed** the discrete-time processes as cadlag processes in $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$ (step functions or linear interpolation).
2. **Verify tightness** using the Aldous-Rebolledo criterion.
3. **Identify the limit** by showing convergence of finite-dimensional distributions, or equivalently, convergence of predictable characteristics.

This is how Jacod and Shiryaev prove their main convergence theorems: convergence of triangular arrays of semimartingales to a semimartingale limit is established by verifying convergence of the characteristic triplets, with tightness provided by the Aldous-Rebolledo framework.

## Skorokhod Representation Theorem

A powerful companion result: if $\mu_n \Rightarrow \mu$ weakly on $\mathbb{D}[0,T]$, then there exist random elements $Y_n, Y$ defined on a common probability space such that $Y_n \sim \mu_n$, $Y \sim \mu$, and $Y_n \to Y$ **almost surely** in the J1 metric.

This allows one to replace weak convergence arguments with almost-sure arguments, often simplifying proofs of continuous mapping theorems.

## The Multidimensional Case

For $\mathbb{R}^d$-valued cadlag processes, one works with $\mathbb{D}([0,T], \mathbb{R}^d)$. The J1 topology generalizes naturally: the same time change $\lambda$ applies to all coordinates simultaneously. This is important in Jacod and Shiryaev's treatment, where processes and their predictable characteristics are often vector-valued.

A subtlety: the product topology on $\mathbb{D}^d$ is **strictly finer** than the J1 topology on $\mathbb{D}(\mathbb{R}^d)$. Convergence in the product topology requires that jump times of each coordinate be matched separately, while J1 on $\mathbb{D}(\mathbb{R}^d)$ uses a single time change for all coordinates. The latter is the correct topology for multivariate limit theorems.

## Applications

**Donsker's theorem.** Convergence of the random walk to Brownian motion: the canonical example of J1 convergence in $\mathbb{D}$, which when the limit is continuous reduces to uniform convergence.

**Convergence to Levy processes.** Triangular arrays of row-independent random variables, embedded as step processes, converge in $\mathbb{D}$ under the J1 topology to a [[levy-processes|Levy process]]. This is the functional analogue of the classical infinitely divisible limit theorem.

**Diffusion approximation.** Sequences of Markov chains (birth-death processes, queueing models) converge in $\mathbb{D}$ to diffusion processes, with tightness established via generator convergence and the Aldous condition.

**Financial mathematics.** Discrete-time trading models converge to continuous-time [[semimartingale-models|semimartingale models]] in the Skorokhod topology, which is the rigorous foundation for the passage from binomial trees to Black-Scholes.
