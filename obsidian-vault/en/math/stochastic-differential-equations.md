# Stochastic Differential Equations

Stochastic differential equations (SDEs) are the language in which continuous-time random dynamics are written. The general Ito SDE:

$$dX_t = b(t, X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t, X_t)\,dW_t,$$

with drift $b$ and diffusion coefficient $\sigma$, models phenomena from financial asset prices to population dynamics to the signal processes in [[nonlinear-filtering|filtering theory]]. Liptser and Shiryaev develop the theory of SDEs as the foundation for the partially observed systems they study: the signal $\theta_t$ and observation $\xi_t$ are both solutions of SDEs, and the properties of these solutions — existence, uniqueness, regularity — determine what can be said about the filter.

The theory divides into two fundamentally different notions of solution: **strong** (pathwise) and **weak** (in law). The interplay between them, crystallized in the Yamada-Watanabe theorem, is one of the elegant structural results of stochastic analysis.

## Strong Solutions

**Definition.** A **strong solution** of the SDE $dX_t = b(t,X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t,X_t)\,dW_t$ on a filtered probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, (\mathcal{F}_t), P)$ carrying a Brownian motion $W$ is a process $X$ such that:

1. $X$ is adapted to the filtration generated by $W$ (and the initial condition $X_0$).
2. $\int_0^T |b(t,X_t)|\,dt + \int_0^T \sigma^2(t,X_t)\,dt < \infty$ a.s. for each $T$.
3. $X_t = X_0 + \int_0^t b(s,X_s)\,ds + \int_0^t \sigma(s,X_s)\,dW_s$ a.s. for all $t$.

The key requirement is adaptedness to $\mathcal{F}_t^W$: the solution is built from the given Brownian motion, path by path.

**Theorem (Ito).** If $b$ and $\sigma$ are globally Lipschitz in $x$, uniformly in $t$:

$$|b(t,x) - b(t,y)| + |\sigma(t,x) - \sigma(t,y)| \leq K|x - y|,$$

and satisfy a linear growth condition $|b(t,x)| + |\sigma(t,x)| \leq K(1 + |x|)$, then for any $\mathcal{F}_0$-measurable $X_0$ with $\mathbb{E}[X_0^2] < \infty$, the SDE has a unique strong solution on $[0, \infty)$.

The proof is by Picard iteration: define $X^{(0)}_t = X_0$ and $X^{(n+1)}_t = X_0 + \int_0^t b(s, X^{(n)}_s)\,ds + \int_0^t \sigma(s, X^{(n)}_s)\,dW_s$. The Lipschitz condition and the Ito isometry give $\mathbb{E}[\sup_{s \leq t}|X^{(n+1)}_s - X^{(n)}_s|^2] \leq C^n t^n / n!$, ensuring convergence.

## Weak Solutions

**Definition.** A **weak solution** consists of a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$, a filtration, a Brownian motion $W$, and a process $X$ on this space satisfying the SDE. The probability space and Brownian motion are part of the solution — they are not given in advance.

Weak solutions are solutions **in law**: two weak solutions are considered the same if $X$ has the same finite-dimensional distributions. This is a strictly weaker notion than strong existence.

**Example (Tanaka).** The SDE $dX_t = \mathrm{sgn}(X_t)\,dW_t$ has a weak solution (namely, any Brownian motion — since $|W_t|$ is a weak solution via Tanaka's formula) but no strong solution. The diffusion coefficient $\sigma(x) = \mathrm{sgn}(x)$ is not Lipschitz at zero; the pathwise map $W \mapsto X$ does not exist.

## The Yamada-Watanabe Theorem

**Theorem (Yamada-Watanabe).** For the SDE $dX_t = b(t,X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t,X_t)\,dW_t$:

$$\text{Weak existence} + \text{Pathwise uniqueness} \implies \text{Strong existence (and uniqueness in law)}.$$

**Pathwise uniqueness** means: on any probability space with any Brownian motion $W$, if $X$ and $Y$ are two solutions with $X_0 = Y_0$ a.s., then $X_t = Y_t$ for all $t$ a.s.

This theorem separates the existence question (which can be attacked by compactness/tightness arguments, using [[weak-convergence-prokhorov|Prokhorov's theorem]] and [[skorokhod-topology|Skorokhod topology]]) from the uniqueness question (which requires analytic estimates on the coefficients). It is one of the most useful structural results in the theory.

## The Martingale Problem

**Definition (Stroock-Varadhan).** The **martingale problem** for the operator $\mathcal{L} = b \cdot \nabla + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \Delta$ is: find a probability measure $P$ on path space $C([0,\infty); \mathbb{R}^d)$ such that for every $f \in C_c^2$:

$$f(X_t) - f(X_0) - \int_0^t \mathcal{L}f(X_s)\,ds$$

is a $P$-martingale with respect to the canonical filtration.

**Theorem.** A weak solution of the SDE exists if and only if the martingale problem for $\mathcal{L}$ has a solution. Uniqueness in law for the SDE corresponds to uniqueness of the martingale problem.

The martingale problem formulation is powerful because it avoids explicit reference to a Brownian motion, making compactness arguments natural. It connects SDE theory to the theory of Markov processes and semigroups.

## One-Dimensional Theory

In dimension one, much sharper results are available.

**Theorem (Yamada-Watanabe, 1-d).** Pathwise uniqueness holds for $dX_t = b(t,X_t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t)\,dW_t$ if $\sigma$ satisfies:

$$|\sigma(x) - \sigma(y)| \leq \rho(|x - y|),$$

where $\int_0^{\varepsilon} \rho^{-2}(u)\,du = \infty$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$, and $b$ is Lipschitz. For instance, $\sigma(x) = |x|^\alpha$ with $\alpha \geq 1/2$ gives pathwise uniqueness (the classical Lipschitz condition requires $\alpha \geq 1$).

**Theorem (Engelbert-Schmidt).** For $dX_t = \sigma(X_t)\,dW_t$ (no drift), a weak solution exists and is unique in law if and only if $\sigma^{-2}$ is locally integrable (i.e., $\int_{x-\varepsilon}^{x+\varepsilon} \sigma^{-2}(y)\,dy < \infty$ for all $x$).

## Comparison Theorems

**Theorem.** Let $X$ and $Y$ solve $dX_t = b_1(t,X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t,X_t)\,dW_t$ and $dY_t = b_2(t,Y_t)\,dt + \sigma(t,Y_t)\,dW_t$ (same diffusion coefficient, same Brownian motion). If $X_0 \leq Y_0$ a.s. and $b_1(t,x) \leq b_2(t,x)$ for all $(t,x)$, then $X_t \leq Y_t$ for all $t$ a.s.

Comparison theorems require that both processes share the same diffusion coefficient. They are essential in financial mathematics (bounding option prices) and in proving properties of the solutions (non-negativity, boundedness).

## Feller's Test for Explosions

For the one-dimensional SDE $dX_t = b(X_t)\,dt + \sigma(X_t)\,dW_t$ on an interval $(l, r)$, Feller's test determines whether the boundaries $l$ and $r$ are reached in finite time. Define the scale function $s(x) = \int^x \exp\bigl(-2\int^y b(z)/\sigma^2(z)\,dz\bigr)\,dy$ and the speed measure $m(dx) = [s'(x)\sigma^2(x)]^{-1}\,dx$. The boundary $r$ is:

- **Natural** (never reached) if $\int^r s'(x)\,dx = \infty$ or $\int^r m(dx)\,\int^x s'(y)\,dy = \infty$.
- **Entrance** (can start from but never returns) or **exit** (reached and absorbed) according to the finiteness of the corresponding integrals.

This classification is fundamental for understanding when solutions are global ($X_t$ exists for all $t$) versus when explosion to the boundary occurs.

## Applications

**Diffusion models in finance.** The [[geometric-brownian-motion|geometric Brownian motion]] $dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t$ (Lipschitz on $(0,\infty)$, strong solution exists) is the foundation of the Black-Scholes theory. The [[heston-model|Heston model]] uses a CIR-type SDE $dV_t = \kappa(\bar{V} - V_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{V_t}\,dW_t$ for volatility — the $\sqrt{V}$ coefficient is Holder-$1/2$, and Yamada-Watanabe (1-d) guarantees pathwise uniqueness.

**Population dynamics.** The Feller diffusion $dX_t = \alpha X_t\,dt + \sigma\sqrt{X_t}\,dW_t$ models population size. Feller's test shows that $X = 0$ is an absorbing boundary (extinction) under certain parameter regimes.

**Filtering theory.** In the signal-observation model of Liptser-Shiryaev, the signal $\theta_t$ satisfies an SDE. The regularity of solutions (moment bounds, Markov property) feeds directly into the well-posedness of the [[nonlinear-filtering|filtering equations]].

**Stochastic control.** Controlled diffusions $dX_t = b(t, X_t, u_t)\,dt + \sigma(t, X_t)\,dW_t$ with admissible controls $u$ are the basis of dynamic programming and the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation.
