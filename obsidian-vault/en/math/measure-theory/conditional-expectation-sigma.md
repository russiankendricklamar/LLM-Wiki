# Conditional Expectation w.r.t. a σ-Algebra

Conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the **single most useful object** in probability theory after probability itself. It is "the best prediction of $X$ using only information from the σ-algebra $\mathcal{G}$." Unlike the elementary definition "given event $B$" (which only works when $\mathbb{P}(B) > 0$), Kolmogorov's version allows conditioning **on any σ-algebra** — even one generated by a continuous random variable (where every individual event has zero probability). of Shiryaev's "Probability" is devoted to it. Without conditional expectation we cannot define a **martingale**, state Fubini's theorem in full, prove the Radon-Nikodym theorem, or formulate [[girsanov-semimartingales|Girsanov]]. It is the technical foundation of all advanced probability.

## Motivation: conditioning on a null event

Elementary definition:

$$\mathbb{P}(A \mid B) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A \cap B)}{\mathbb{P}(B)}, \quad \text{provided } \mathbb{P}(B) > 0.$$

This works while $\mathbb{P}(B) > 0$. But what about "find the distribution of $X$ given $Y = y$" when $Y$ is continuous? Then $\mathbb{P}(Y = y) = 0$ for every $y$ and the formula is undefined.

Kolmogorov in 1933 bypassed this **globally**, refusing to define "$X$ given $Y = y$" for each $y$ separately and instead defining a single random variable $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \sigma(Y)]$ that "carries all conditional information at once."

## Definition

**Definition.** Let $X$ be an integrable random variable ($\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty$) on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$, and let $\mathcal{G} \subset \mathcal{F}$ be a sub-σ-algebra. The **conditional expectation** $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is any random variable $Y$ such that:

1. $Y$ is **measurable with respect to $\mathcal{G}$**.
2. $\int_A Y \, d\mathbb{P} = \int_A X \, d\mathbb{P}$ for every $A \in \mathcal{G}$.

**Claim (Radon-Nikodym).** Such a random variable $Y$ exists and is unique almost surely.

Existence follows from the Radon-Nikodym theorem (the measure $A \mapsto \int_A X \, d\mathbb{P}$ is absolutely continuous w.r.t. $\mathbb{P}$ restricted to $\mathcal{G}$). Uniqueness follows because two functions with equal integrals over every $A \in \mathcal{G}$ are a.s. equal on $\mathcal{G}$.

## Intuition

Condition 1 ($\mathcal{G}$-measurability) means: the value of $Y$ is fully determined by the information in $\mathcal{G}$. Knowing only $\mathcal{G}$, you could compute $Y$.

Condition 2 ("the average of $Y$ matches $X$ on every $\mathcal{G}$-event") means: on any subset that $\mathcal{G}$ can distinguish, $Y$ matches $X$ **on average**. Within each "indistinguishability class" of $\mathcal{G}$, $Y$ is the **average** of $X$ over that class.

This is exactly "the best averaging of $X$ using only information from $\mathcal{G}$."

## L² projection

If $X \in L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$, then $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the **orthogonal projection** of $X$ onto the subspace $L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{G}, \mathbb{P})$ ($\mathcal{G}$-measurable functions). That is,

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = \arg\min_{Y \in L^2(\mathcal{G})} \mathbb{E}\big[(X - Y)^2\big].$$

The **best mean-square predictor** of $X$ among all functions using only information from $\mathcal{G}$.

In machine learning: if $\mathcal{G} = \sigma(Y)$ is the σ-algebra generated by features $Y$, then $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the ideal (but generally non-computable) predictor of $X$ from $Y$. Every regression is an attempt to approximate this function.

## Properties

Conditional expectation satisfies all the usual properties of expectation plus a few new ones:

1. **Linearity**: $\mathbb{E}[aX + bY \mid \mathcal{G}] = a \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}] + b \mathbb{E}[Y \mid \mathcal{G}]$.
2. **Tower of total expectation**: $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ — law of total expectation.
3. **"Pull out what is known"**: if $Y$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable, then $\mathbb{E}[XY \mid \mathcal{G}] = Y \cdot \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$.
4. **Tower property**: if $\mathcal{H} \subset \mathcal{G}$, then $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{H}]$ — "the smaller information wins."
5. **Independence**: if $X$ is independent of $\mathcal{G}$, then $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ — a constant.
6. **Jensen's inequality**: for convex $\varphi$, $\varphi(\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]) \leq \mathbb{E}[\varphi(X) \mid \mathcal{G}]$.
7. **Contractivity**: $\|\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]\|_p \leq \|X\|_p$ for $p \geq 1$.

The tower property (4) is the most-used in martingale theory: it lets you "slide" between smaller and larger σ-algebras.

## Connection to martingales

A stochastic process $(X_n)_{n \geq 0}$ is a **martingale** w.r.t. a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_n)$ if:

1. $X_n$ is adapted: $X_n$ is $\mathcal{F}_n$-measurable.
2. $X_n$ is integrable: $\mathbb{E}|X_n| < \infty$.
3. **Martingale property**: $\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = X_n$.

That is, "the best prediction of the next value given all available information equals the current value." This is the mathematically precise expression of "fair game": expected profit in the next round is zero.

Without conditional expectation w.r.t. a σ-algebra this definition would **not make sense** for general filtrations. Kolmogorov's construction is what makes martingales into a real mathematical object.

See [[discrete-martingales]] for the full development of the theory.

## Regular conditional probability

Conditional on $\mathcal{G}$ one can speak of the **conditional distribution** of a random variable $X$. There exists a function $\mu_X(\cdot \mid \mathcal{G})(\omega)$ — for almost every $\omega$ — that is a probability measure on $\mathbb{R}$ for which

$$\mathbb{E}[f(X) \mid \mathcal{G}](\omega) = \int_\mathbb{R} f(x) \, \mu_X(dx \mid \mathcal{G})(\omega).$$

This is the **regular conditional probability**. It exists for a wide class of spaces (including all Borel ones) — and it is what allows speaking of "the distribution of $X$ given $Y = y$" in a strict sense, bypassing zero denominators.

## In finance

Conditional expectation is the workhorse of financial mathematics:

- Option price: $C_t = \mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q}[H \mid \mathcal{F}_t] / B_t$ — conditional expectation of payoff under the martingale measure.
- Greeks (delta, gamma): derivatives of the conditional expectation w.r.t. parameters.
- Hedging: replicating portfolio is the decomposition of the martingale $\mathbb{E}^\mathbb{Q}[H \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$ via a stochastic integral.
- Stochastic filtering (Kalman): $\hat{X}_t = \mathbb{E}[X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t^{\text{obs}}]$ — optimal state estimate from observations.
