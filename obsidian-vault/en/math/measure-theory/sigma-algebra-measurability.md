# σ-Algebras and Measurability

A σ-algebra is the formalization of "information": which subsets of the sample space we **can distinguish** at the current state of knowledge. In [[kolmogorov-probability-axioms|Kolmogorov's axiomatization]] probability is defined not on every subset of $\Omega$ but only on elements of a σ-algebra $\mathcal{F}$. This technical restriction turns out to be deeply meaningful: a σ-algebra carries the meaning of "what we know," and the time-evolution of σ-algebras is what financial mathematics calls a **filtration**.

This topic fills of Shiryaev's "Probability". Without an understanding of σ-algebras, a correct definition of [[conditional-expectation-sigma|conditional expectation]] is impossible — and without that, no martingale theory, no FTAP, no stochastic calculus.

## Definition

**Definition.** A family $\mathcal{F}$ of subsets of a set $\Omega$ is a **σ-algebra** if:

1. $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$.
2. **Closure under complements**: $A \in \mathcal{F} \Rightarrow A^c \in \mathcal{F}$.
3. **Closure under countable unions**: $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F} \Rightarrow \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i \in \mathcal{F}$.

These properties automatically imply $\varnothing \in \mathcal{F}$ and closure under countable intersections (de Morgan).

The "σ" (from German *Summe*, sum) emphasises countable rather than merely finite closure. Requiring only finite unions yields an **algebra**, which is significantly weaker.

## Simplest examples

- **Trivial σ-algebra**: $\mathcal{F} = \{\varnothing, \Omega\}$. The minimal one. It knows only "everything or nothing." Any random variable measurable with respect to it is constant.
- **Power-set σ-algebra**: $\mathcal{F} = 2^\Omega$. Knows everything — every subset. Suitable only for discrete $\Omega$.
- **Borel σ-algebra** $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ — the smallest σ-algebra on $\mathbb{R}$ containing all open intervals. The **standard** choice for continuous distributions.

## Generated σ-algebra

**Definition.** For an arbitrary family $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets of $\Omega$ there exists the **smallest** σ-algebra containing $\mathcal{C}$, denoted $\sigma(\mathcal{C})$:

$$\sigma(\mathcal{C}) = \bigcap \big\{ \mathcal{F} : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a σ-algebra}, \mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{F} \big\}.$$

This intersection of all σ-algebras containing $\mathcal{C}$ is itself a σ-algebra (easy to verify).

**Examples.**
- $\sigma(\{(a, b) : a < b\}) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ — the Borel σ-algebra is generated by intervals.
- For a random variable $X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$, the **σ-algebra generated by $X$** is $\sigma(X) = \{X^{-1}(B) : B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\}$. This is "all the information contained in the value of $X$." Knowing the realization of $X$ lets us distinguish only those events lying in $\sigma(X)$.

## Measurable functions

**Definition.** A function $f : (\Omega_1, \mathcal{F}_1) \to (\Omega_2, \mathcal{F}_2)$ is **measurable** if

$$f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}_1 \quad \forall B \in \mathcal{F}_2.$$

That is, the preimage of every "measurable" set in the second space lies in the σ-algebra of the first.

A **random variable** is exactly a measurable function $X : (\Omega, \mathcal{F}) \to (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$. The measurability condition guarantees that the question "$X \leq a$" makes sense — the set $\{X \leq a\} = X^{-1}((-\infty, a])$ lies in $\mathcal{F}$ and can be assigned a probability.

## Adaptedness and filtration

A **filtration** is an increasing family of σ-algebras $\mathcal{F}_0 \subset \mathcal{F}_1 \subset \mathcal{F}_2 \subset \ldots$ Intuition: $\mathcal{F}_t$ is "all the information available at time $t$." Time passes, knowledge accumulates, the σ-algebra grows.

A stochastic process $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is **adapted** to a filtration $(\mathcal{F}_t)$ if $X_t$ is measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_t$ for every $t$. The value of $X_t$ can be "read off" the information available at time $t$ — no peeking into the future.

This conceptual minimum is the foundation of all stochastic calculus and financial mathematics:

- **Adaptedness** — no trading on the future.
- **Predictability** ($X_t$ measurable w.r.t. $\mathcal{F}_{t-1}$) — strategy is determined by information **strictly before** the current moment.
- **Stochastic integral** $\int H \, dM$ is defined only for predictable integrand $H$ against a martingale $M$ — otherwise there would be no ban on "insider trading."

## Information interpretation

It is useful to picture a σ-algebra as a **partition** of $\Omega$ into indistinguishability classes: two outcomes $\omega_1, \omega_2$ are "$\mathcal{F}$-equivalent" if for every $A \in \mathcal{F}$ either both lie in $A$ or both do not. The smaller the σ-algebra, the coarser the partition, the less you can distinguish.

This picture makes it clear why σ-algebras "grow" with time (you receive more information) and why [[conditional-expectation-sigma|conditional expectation w.r.t. a σ-algebra]] is "the best prediction of $X$ using only information from $\mathcal{F}$."

## π-λ systems and Dynkin's theorem

The technical workhorse for σ-algebra arguments is **Dynkin's π-λ systems**. A **π-system** is closed under finite intersections. A **λ-system** is closed under complements and countable unions of **disjoint** sets.

**Dynkin's theorem (π-λ).** If a π-system $\mathcal{P}$ is contained in a λ-system $\mathcal{L}$, then $\sigma(\mathcal{P}) \subset \mathcal{L}$.

This is the workhorse of many proofs: to show that **every** Borel set has some property, it suffices to show that
(1) the property holds on intervals (a π-system),
(2) the set on which the property holds is a λ-system.

Shiryaev uses this method at every step — for uniqueness of measure extension, proofs of independence, etc.

## Connection to information theory

In [[shannon-entropy|Shannon's information theory]] a σ-algebra plays the role of "the set of questions to which we can get an answer." The entropy of a random variable conditional on a σ-algebra is **the measure of remaining uncertainty** after that information.

In machine learning this becomes the concept of **"representation"**: an embedding of an input $X$ is a sub-σ-algebra w.r.t. which we form a prediction. The richer the embedding (larger σ-algebra), the more accurately we can predict — but the greater the overfitting risk.
