# Product Measures and Fubini's Theorem

Product measures lie at the intersection of probability theory, harmonic analysis, and quantitative finance. They formalize the notion of "independent" sources of randomness and enable the reduction of double integrals to iterated integrals—a principle that is as fundamental to modern analysis as the fundamental theorem of calculus. Fubini's theorem is the precise statement: under appropriate integrability conditions, the order of integration does not matter.

Let $(X_1, \mathcal{F}_1, \mu_1)$ and $(X_2, \mathcal{F}_2, \mu_2)$ be two measure spaces.

## Product σ-Algebra

**Definition.** The **product σ-algebra** $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$ is the σ-algebra generated by the collection of rectangles (or product sets):
$$\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2 = \sigma\left\{ A \times B : A \in \mathcal{F}_1, B \in \mathcal{F}_2 \right\}.$$

Any set in $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$ is called **measurable with respect to the product σ-algebra**. The σ-algebra is the smallest σ-algebra containing all rectangles.

**Key observation:** A measurable set $E \in \mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$ on the product space need not have a simple rectangular structure. For example, $E$ could be a diagonal, a curve, or a complicated fractal-like subset. The σ-algebra accommodates all countable unions and complements of rectangles.

## Existence and Uniqueness of Product Measure

**Theorem (Carathéodory Extension).** Suppose $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ are σ-finite measures on $(X_1, \mathcal{F}_1)$ and $(X_2, \mathcal{F}_2)$ respectively. Then there exists a unique measure $\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2$ on $(X_1 \times X_2, \mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2)$ such that
$$(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2)(A \times B) = \mu_1(A) \cdot \mu_2(B) \quad \text{for all} \quad A \in \mathcal{F}_1, \, B \in \mathcal{F}_2.$$

The measure $\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2$ is called the **product measure**.

**Proof sketch:** Begin with the product σ-algebra generated by rectangles. Define the set function $\lambda(A \times B) = \mu_1(A) \mu_2(B)$ on measurable rectangles. This is a pre-measure (countably additive on disjoint rectangles). By the Carathéodory extension theorem, extend $\lambda$ uniquely to a measure on $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$. σ-finiteness of $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ ensures the extension is unique.

## Fubini's Theorem

**Theorem (Fubini).** Let $f : X_1 \times X_2 \to \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$) be a measurable function with respect to $\mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2$.

1. **If $f \geq 0$** (non-negative case), then
   $$\int_{X_1 \times X_2} f \, d(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2) = \int_{X_1} \left( \int_{X_2} f(x, y) \, d\mu_2(y) \right) d\mu_1(x) = \int_{X_2} \left( \int_{X_1} f(x, y) \, d\mu_1(x) \right) d\mu_2(y).$$

2. **If $f$ is integrable** (i.e., $\int |f| \, d(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2) < ∞$), then the iterated integrals above converge absolutely and equal the double integral.

In both cases, the iterated integral over the "outside" variable is well-defined: for almost every $x$ (with respect to $\mu_1$), the inner integral $\int_{X_2} f(x, y) \, d\mu_2(y)$ exists, and the outer integral of this function is finite.

## Tonelli's Theorem

**Theorem (Tonelli).** If $f : X_1 \times X_2 \to [0, ∞]$ is measurable and non-negative, then
$$\int f \, d(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2) = \int \left( \int f(x, y) \, d\mu_2(y) \right) d\mu_1(x).$$

Tonelli's theorem applies without any integrability assumption: the function need only be non-negative and measurable. This is powerful for proving results about arbitrary measurable sets and for computing outer measures.

**Remark:** Tonelli and Fubini are often stated together as "Fubini-Tonelli," with Tonelli addressing the non-negative case (where no integrability assumption is needed) and Fubini the integrable case (where order of integration is guaranteed to interchange).

## Necessity of Integrability

**Counterexample (Fubini failure).** Let $X_1 = X_2 = [0, 1]$ with Lebesgue measure. Define
$$f(x, y) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x > y \\ -1 & \text{if } x < y \\ 0 & \text{if } x = y \end{cases}$$

Then:
- $\int_0^1 \left( \int_0^1 f(x, y) \, dy \right) dx = 0$ (the iterated integral over $x$ first)
- $\int_0^1 \left( \int_0^1 f(x, y) \, dx \right) dy = 0$ (the iterated integral over $y$ first)

However, $\int_{[0,1]^2} |f| \, d(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2) = ∞$, so $f$ is not integrable. The coincidence of both iterated integrals is misleading; in general, non-integrable functions can produce different iterated integrals depending on order.

## Infinite Product Measures and Kolmogorov Extension Theorem

For countably many σ-finite measure spaces $(X_n, \mathcal{F}_n, \mu_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$, one can construct the infinite product measure
$$\bigotimes_{n=1}^{\infty} \mu_n \quad \text{on} \quad \bigotimes_{n=1}^{\infty} \mathcal{F}_n.$$

**Kolmogorov Extension Theorem.** On a product space $\prod_{n=1}^{\infty} X_n$ (typically $\mathbb{R}^{\mathbb{N}}$), a probability measure on the product σ-algebra is uniquely determined by its finite-dimensional marginals: the distributions of $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ for all $n$. This is the theoretical foundation for stochastic processes: specifying the law of each $(X_t)$ marginal fixes a unique process distribution on the path space.

## Independence and Product Measure Factorization

Two events $A \in \mathcal{F}_1$ and $B \in \mathcal{F}_2$ are **independent** with respect to a product measure if
$$(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2)(A \times B) = \mu_1(A) \cdot \mu_2(B).$$

More broadly, a product measure encodes independence: random variables $X_1$ and $X_2$ taking values in $X_1$ and $X_2$ are independent if and only if their joint distribution equals the product of their marginal distributions. This is the probabilistic interpretation of Fubini: independence allows one to compute joint expectations as products of marginal expectations.

## Tensor Products in Hilbert Spaces

The product measure perspective extends to functional analysis. The **tensor product Hilbert space** $L^2(\mu_1) \otimes L^2(\mu_2)$ is (isometrically) isomorphic to $L^2(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2)$ via
$$f \otimes g \mapsto (x, y) \mapsto f(x) g(y).$$

The inner product on $L^2(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2)$ satisfies
$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int f \bar{g} \, d(\mu_1 \otimes \mu_2) = \int \left( \int f(x, y) \overline{g(x, y)} \, d\mu_2(y) \right) d\mu_1(x).$$

This is essential in quantum mechanics (separable Hilbert spaces for composite systems) and in functional data analysis.

## Convolution and Characteristic Functions

**Convolution.** For functions $f, g \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, their convolution is
$$(f * g)(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x - y) g(y) \, dy.$$

By Fubini, $f * g \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ with
$$\|f * g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1 \|g\|_1.$$

Fubini underlies this estimate: applying the theorem to the function $F(x, y) = f(x - y) g(y)$ over $\mathbb{R}^2$ and substituting $u = x - y$ yields the convolution inequality.

**Characteristic Functions.** For a random variable $X$ with distribution $\mu$, the characteristic function is
$$\phi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}] = \int e^{itx} \, d\mu(x).$$

For independent random variables $X, Y$ with product law $\mu = \mu_X \otimes \mu_Y$, the characteristic function of $X + Y$ factors as
$$\phi_{X+Y}(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{it(X+Y)}] = \int \int e^{it(x+y)} \, d\mu_X(x) \, d\mu_Y(y) = \phi_X(t) \phi_Y(t).$$

This follows immediately from Fubini applied to the product measure.

---

**Essential properties:**
- **σ-finiteness requirement:** necessary for Carathéodory extension; product measures of non-σ-finite measures may not be unique
- **Measurability:** before applying Fubini, verify that $f$ is measurable with respect to the product σ-algebra (often automatic from standard constructions)
- **Integration order:** Tonelli works for any non-negative measurable $f$; Fubini requires $|f|$ to be integrable for order interchange
