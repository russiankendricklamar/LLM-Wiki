# Martingale Representation Theorem

The martingale representation theorem (MRT) is one of the deepest structural results in stochastic analysis: it asserts that **every square-integrable martingale adapted to the Brownian filtration is a stochastic integral with respect to the Brownian motion**. This result, developed in detail by Liptser and Shiryaev in the context of filtering and statistics of random processes, has far-reaching consequences — from the completeness of financial markets to the representation of functionals of the Wiener process. The theorem reveals that the Brownian filtration carries no "extra randomness" beyond what is generated by the Brownian motion itself.

## The Representation Theorem

Let $W = (W_t)_{t \in [0,T]}$ be a standard Brownian motion on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ and let $\mathbb{F}^W = (\mathcal{F}^W_t)$ be its natural filtration (augmented by null sets).

**Theorem (Martingale Representation).** If $M = (M_t)_{t \in [0,T]}$ is a square-integrable martingale adapted to $\mathbb{F}^W$, then there exists a unique predictable process $\varphi \in L^2(W)$ such that

$$M_t = M_0 + \int_0^t \varphi_s \, dW_s, \quad 0 \leq t \leq T.$$

Equivalently, every $\mathcal{F}^W_T$-measurable random variable $F \in L^2(\Omega)$ admits the representation

$$F = \mathbb{E}[F] + \int_0^T \varphi_s \, dW_s.$$

The process $\varphi$ is called the **integrand representation** of $F$.

## Proof Strategy

The classical proof proceeds in two stages. First, one shows that the family of stochastic exponentials

$$\mathcal{E}_t(\theta) = \exp\!\left(\theta W_t - \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^2 t\right), \quad \theta \in \mathbb{R},$$

is **total** in $L^2(\mathcal{F}^W_T)$ — that is, finite linear combinations are dense. Since each $\mathcal{E}(\theta)$ is an Ito integral (apply [[doleans-dade-exponential|Doleans-Dade's formula]]: $d\mathcal{E}_t = \theta \mathcal{E}_t \, dW_t$), any $L^2$-limit of such combinations inherits the integral representation. The second stage extends from the dense set to all of $L^2$ by an isometry argument using the Ito isometry $\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\int \varphi \, dW\right)^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\int \varphi^2 \, dt\right]$.

An alternative approach uses the **completeness of the Brownian filtration**: the predictable $\sigma$-algebra on $[0,T] \times \Omega$ is generated by $W$ alone, so every predictable process is a measurable function of the Brownian path.

## The Clark-Ocone Formula

The MRT guarantees existence of $\varphi$ but does not give an explicit formula. The **Clark-Ocone formula** from Malliavin calculus fills this gap.

**Theorem (Clark-Ocone).** If $F \in \mathbb{D}^{1,2}$ (the Malliavin-Sobolev space), then

$$F = \mathbb{E}[F] + \int_0^T \mathbb{E}[D_t F \mid \mathcal{F}_t] \, dW_t,$$

where $D_t F$ is the **Malliavin derivative** of $F$ at time $t$.

Thus the integrand is $\varphi_t = \mathbb{E}[D_t F \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$. For example, if $F = g(W_T)$ with $g$ smooth, then $D_t F = g'(W_T)$ for all $t \leq T$, and $\varphi_t = \mathbb{E}[g'(W_T) \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$ — a computable conditional expectation.

## Multiple Brownian Motions

The theorem extends naturally to the multi-dimensional case. If $W = (W^1, \ldots, W^d)$ is a $d$-dimensional Brownian motion, then every $\mathbb{F}^W$-martingale $M \in L^2$ has the representation

$$M_t = M_0 + \sum_{i=1}^d \int_0^t \varphi^i_s \, dW^i_s$$

with a unique vector of predictable integrands $(\varphi^1, \ldots, \varphi^d)$.

## Market Completeness

The MRT is the mathematical engine behind **market completeness** in the [[black-scholes|Black-Scholes]] model. Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, the discounted price of any contingent claim $H$ satisfies $e^{-rT}H \in L^2(\mathcal{F}^W_T)$, so by the MRT:

$$e^{-rT}H = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{-rT}H] + \int_0^T \varphi_s \, dW_s.$$

Translating $\varphi$ back into units of the stock gives a self-financing replicating strategy. Every claim is **attainable** — this is completeness. The [[girsanov-semimartingales|Girsanov theorem]] ensures the measure change preserves the Brownian structure, so the representation carries over from $\mathbb{P}$ to $\mathbb{Q}$.

## Failure Beyond Brownian Filtrations

The MRT is specific to Brownian (or, more generally, continuous martingale) filtrations. For filtrations generated by processes with jumps — such as [[levy-processes|Levy processes]] or [[hidden-markov-models|point processes]] — the representation theorem **fails**: there exist martingales that cannot be written as stochastic integrals with respect to the driving process. The gap between the space of stochastic integrals and the space of all martingales is precisely the source of **market incompleteness** in jump-diffusion models, where perfect replication of every claim is impossible.

## Connection to Filtering Theory

In the filtering framework of Liptser and Shiryaev, the MRT connects to the **[[innovation-process|innovation process]]**. The innovation $\nu_t = Y_t - \int_0^t \hat{a}_s \, ds$ (where $\hat{a}$ is the optimal filter estimate) is a Brownian motion in the observation filtration. Any square-integrable observation-adapted martingale is then a stochastic integral with respect to $\nu$ — this is the **innovation representation**, the filtering-theory counterpart of the MRT.

## Applications

**Hedging and replication.** The MRT provides the theoretical foundation for delta hedging: the integrand $\varphi$ is the hedge ratio.

**Backward SDEs.** The representation $Y_t = \mathbb{E}[F \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = Y_0 + \int_0^t Z_s \, dW_s$ is the starting point for the theory of BSDEs, where $Z$ plays the role of the control process.

**Wiener functionals.** The Wiener-Ito chaos expansion $F = \sum_{n=0}^\infty I_n(f_n)$ refines the MRT: the first chaos $I_1(f_1) = \int f_1 \, dW$ recovers the representation, while higher chaoses capture the nonlinear structure.
